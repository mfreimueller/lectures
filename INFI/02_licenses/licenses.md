# LECTURE NOTES: License Models for Online Media

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
- Identify at least four different types of media license models (proprietary, open source, Creative Commons, public domain)
- Explain when each license type is appropriate for use in professional contexts
- Evaluate media content to determine its licensing restrictions before use
- Apply proper attribution requirements according to specific license terms

---

## Pre-Lecture Preparation
**Required Reading**: Students should be familiar with basic concepts of copyright and intellectual property (covered in prior IT law or digital media modules)  
**Prerequisite Knowledge**: Understanding of what "copyright" means, basic computer file formats  
**Pre-Class Assignment**: Bring one example of online media they've used for a school project and note if you know its license type

---

## Key Vocabulary & Definitions

| Term | Definition | Context/Example |
|------|------------|-----------------|
| Copyright | Legal protection for original creative works | Automatically applies to photos, text, music created by someone |
| License Model | Terms that define how others may use copyrighted material | Determines if you can modify, share, or sell the work |
| Creative Commons (CC) | Family of licenses allowing flexible sharing with attribution requirements | CC BY allows reuse with credit given |
| Public Domain | Works not protected by copyright; free for anyone to use without restrictions | Very old books, government documents in some countries |
| Proprietary License | All rights reserved; requires explicit permission for any use | Stock photos from Shutterstock, Adobe Stock |
| Attribution (BY) | Requirement to credit the original creator when using their work | Must include author name and source link |
| Share-Alike (SA) | If you modify the work, your new version must have same license terms | CC BY-SA requires derivative works use same license |

## Lecture Content

### Section 1: Introduction to Media Licensing (5 minutes)

**Key Points**: 
- Why licensing matters in professional and academic contexts
- Legal consequences of using media without proper permissions
- The balance between creator rights and public access

**Teaching Notes**: Start with a relatable example—show an image used improperly on social media. Ask students if they've ever wondered whether they can use images from Google Images for their projects. Emphasize that HTL graduates will work in professional environments where these decisions have legal and financial consequences.

**Example/Case Study**: 
Show the 2014 case of a small business fined €5,000+ for using copyrighted stock photos without licenses. Discuss how this could affect any student starting their own business after graduation.

### Section 2: Four Main License Types (12 minutes)

**Key Points**: 
- **Proprietary/Paid Licenses**: Commercial use requires purchase; strict terms
- **Creative Commons**: Six variations with different permissions
- **Open Source/Open Content**: Free to use, modify, distribute (with conditions)
- **Public Domain**: No restrictions whatsoever

**Teaching Notes**: Use a visual comparison chart. Highlight that Creative Commons is not "free of rules"—it has specific requirements. Common misconception: students often think CC = free for everything. Clarify the difference between CC0 and other CC licenses.

**Example/Case Study**: 
Compare two images from Wikimedia Commons (CC BY) vs. a Shutterstock image (proprietary). Show how attribution looks different for each. Discuss when an HTL student might choose one over another based on project requirements and budget.


https://choosealicense.com/

---

### Section 3: Understanding Creative Commons Variations (10 minutes)

**Key Points**: 
- **BY** - Attribution required
- **NC** - Non-commercial only (cannot be used for profit-making projects)
- **ND** - No Derivatives (cannot modify the work)
- **SA** - Share Alike (must license derivatives same way)
- Combinations: CC BY, CC BY-SA, CC BY-NC, etc.

**Teaching Notes**: Use a decision tree visual showing how to choose licenses for your own creations AND how to evaluate others' works. Many students will create content themselves in their careers—help them understand licensing from both perspectives (user and creator).

**Example/Case Study**: 
Present three scenarios:
1. School presentation with photos → CC BY-NC acceptable
2. Commercial portfolio website using images → Must use CC BY or purchased licenses only
3. Modified educational video remixing clips → Check if SA license applies to source material

---

### Section 4: Practical Application & Attribution (8 minutes)

**Key Points**: 
- How to properly attribute media according to license requirements
- Where and how to include attribution (footnotes, credits page, metadata)
- Tools for finding licensed content (Flickr CC, Wikimedia Commons, Unsplash, etc.)

**Teaching Notes**: Walk through the actual process of finding an image on a CC platform. Show them how to read the license information before downloading. Emphasize that attribution isn't just "nice to have"—it's legally required for most licenses except public domain/CC0.

**Example/Case Study**: 
Demonstrate proper vs. improper attribution side-by-side:
- **Proper**: "Photo by [Name] from [Source], licensed under CC BY 4.0" with link
- **Improper**: Just saying "Image from Google Images" or no credit at all

---

### Section 5: Common Pitfalls & Best Practices (5 minutes)

**Key Points**: 
- Don't assume social media images are free to use
- Check license terms even on platforms that seem permissive
- When in doubt, contact the creator directly
- Keep records of licenses and attribution for all materials used

**Teaching Notes**: Address common student misconceptions: "I found it online so it's free," or "I gave credit so I don't need permission." Emphasize that these are legally incorrect assumptions. Share a quick checklist students can use before using any media in their work.

**Example/Case Study**: 
Show a screenshot of a typical license violation notice from YouTube (Content ID claim) and explain what steps to take if it happens. Discuss how this affects professional credibility.

## Student Activities & Discussion

### In-Class Activity 1: License Detective (8 minutes)
**Time Required**: 8 minutes  
**Instructions**: Work in pairs. Each pair receives 3 URLs to online media items with different licenses. Analyze each item and determine: What license does it have? Can you use it for a school project? For commercial work? How do you attribute it correctly? Present findings to class.

**Learning Goal**: Reinforces practical evaluation of licensing terms in real-world scenarios

### Discussion Questions:
1. Why might some creators choose not to release their work under Creative Commons licenses? What are the benefits and drawbacks for both creators and users?
2. How do license models affect innovation and collaboration in digital media production? Can restrictive licenses slow down creative progress?
3. If you were creating educational content for your HTL peers, which license would you choose and why?

---

## Summary & Key Takeaways

- **Core Concept 1**: All online media has a licensing model—never assume something is free to use without checking
- **Core Concept 2**: Creative Commons licenses have specific requirements that must be followed (especially attribution)
- **Connection Points**: Understanding license models connects legal knowledge with practical digital skills needed in modern careers

## Assessment Materials

### Quick Quiz (5 questions):
1. Which of the following media can you use for a commercial project without paying?
   - A) CC BY-NC image
   - B) Shutterstock photo  
   - C) Public domain photograph
   - D) All of the above
   
2. What does "CC BY" require when using someone's work?

3. True or False: If you give credit to an author, you don't need permission to use their copyrighted image.

4. Which Creative Commons license requires that modified works use the same license terms?
   - A) CC BY-ND
   - B) CC BY-SA
   - C) CC0
   - D) All Rights Reserved
   
5. Where is one reliable source for finding media with clear licensing information?

**Answer Key**: 
1. C | 2. Attribution/credit to the original creator | 3. False (attribution alone doesn't grant permission unless license allows it) | 4. B | 5. Wikimedia Commons, Flickr CC search, Unsplash, etc.

## Teaching Tips for Instructor

 **Common Misconceptions to Address Early**:
- "Google Images = free to use" (incorrect)
- "Giving credit means I don't need permission" (incorrect for many licenses)
- "CC0 is the same as public domain" (technically different but functionally similar in practice)

 **HTL-Specific Considerations**: Many HTL students will pursue technical careers where they'll manage digital assets, create presentations, or develop websites. Emphasize practical application over theoretical legal knowledge.
